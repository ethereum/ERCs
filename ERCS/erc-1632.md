## Abstract

This ERC defines a programmable fulfillment assurance primitive for Agent commerce. Building on the Job lifecycle data from [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md), Agent Assurance Protocol (AAP) introduces three core primitives: `AssuranceAccount`, `JobAssurance`, and `Claim`. AAP MAY optionally integrate with [ERC-8004](./eip-8004.md)-compatible identity and reputation infrastructure but does not depend on that standard.

An `AssuranceAccount` is a per-Agent, account-level reserve of self-funded collateral. A `JobAssurance` is a commitment allocation made by an Agent against a specific [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Job. Each JobAssurance is associated with at most one Claim. A `Claim` is a payout request initiated by the Beneficiary when coverage conditions are met; upon approval, the payout is debited from the AssuranceAccount.

AAP is not a pooled underwriting model. It is a self-funded collateral model in which each Assured Agent locks its own capital as a fulfillment guarantee. Payouts are sourced from the Agent's own AssuranceAccount rather than from third-party liquidity pools.

This specification assumes that each AssuranceAccount uses a single ERC-20 settlement asset and defines only the minimum surface required for interoperability. Collateral pricing, AML screening, and advanced resolution mechanisms are defined as optional extensions.

## Motivation

[ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) standardizes Job execution and settlement in Agent commerce but does not standardize programmable risk transfer. As autonomous Agent activity extends into higher-value scenarios, this gap creates concrete problems:

**High-value Agent-to-Agent service delivery.** An Agent delegates a $10,000 data-analysis task to another provider Agent. If the provider fails to deliver by the deadline, the Job expires. The client receives a refund but has already lost opportunity cost and downstream commitments that depended on that result. With AAP, the provider can pre-commit collateral to that Job; if the Job fails, the client can claim compensation from that collateral.

**Cross-organization collaborative tasks.** Multiple Agents collaborate on a complex deliverable. A single provider's failure can delay the entire workflow. AAP allows each participant to post self-funded collateral for the segment it is responsible for, isolating risk at the individual Job level without introducing a shared funding pool across participants.

**Evaluator attestation risk.** If an evaluator incorrectly rejects a valid submission or incorrectly approves a fraudulent submission, the affected party has no recourse path within [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md). An `EvaluatorDispute` JobAssurance provides a structured compensation path, with the payout sourced from the responsible party's own collateral.

**Post-completion settlement failure.** A Job has been completed and attested, but funds fail to release due to reasons attributable to the Assured Agent. A `SettlementDefault` JobAssurance covers such blockage scenarios where the Assured Agent bears responsibility for the settlement process. Settlement failures caused by third-party contract defects or platform infrastructure outages are not within the scope of this coverage type.

Agents lack legal personality and cannot access traditional surety or guarantee markets. AAP encodes fulfillment guarantee logic directly into smart contracts. By standardizing AssuranceAccount, JobAssurance, and Claim primitives, AAP enables interoperable assurance registration and claims tooling across different implementations.

Assurance participation data may also be consumed by external reputation or compliance systems, but such integrations are outside the scope of this specification.

### Glossary

This section defines core terms used in this specification and their relationship to the [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) terminology.

**Terms inherited from ERC-8183 (used as-is in AAP):**

- **Provider**: The Agent that executes and delivers work output in an ERC-8183 Job. In AAP, a Provider typically serves as the Assured Agent.
- **Client**: The party that commissions and pays for a Job in ERC-8183. In AAP, the Client typically serves as the Beneficiary.
- **Evaluator**: The party responsible for validating Job deliverables in ERC-8183. This role is distinct from the AAP Claims Resolver; the `EvaluatorDispute` coverage type specifically addresses scenarios where an Evaluator's decision is disputed.
- **Job**: The task execution unit defined by ERC-8183, with a complete on-chain lifecycle. Each AAP JobAssurance maps one-to-one to an ERC-8183 Job.

**New terms introduced by AAP:**

- **Assured Agent**: The party that deposits collateral into the AAP contract and makes assurance commitments against specific Jobs. For `JobFailure` and `SettlementDefault`, this is typically the ERC-8183 Provider; for `EvaluatorDispute`, this is typically the Evaluator whose decision is disputed.
- **Beneficiary**: The sole payout recipient designated by the Assured Agent at `commitToJob()` time. For `JobFailure` and `SettlementDefault`, this is typically the ERC-8183 Client; for `EvaluatorDispute`, this is the affected party (Client or Provider, depending on the dispute direction).
- **Claims Resolver**: The party responsible for adjudicating Claims in AAP, independent of the ERC-8183 Evaluator.
- **AssuranceAccount**: The account-level reserve of self-funded collateral maintained by the Assured Agent within the AAP contract.
- **JobAssurance**: A commitment allocation carved out from the AssuranceAccount by the Assured Agent for a specific Job.
- **Claim**: A payout request initiated by the Beneficiary when coverage conditions are met.

### Roles and Participation

AAP comprises three core roles:

**Assured Agent**: The party that self-deposits collateral into the AAP contract and makes assurance commitments against specific Jobs. By locking its own assets, the Assured Agent demonstrates on-chain-verifiable fulfillment guarantee capability to counterparties.

The mapping between ERC-8183 roles and AAP roles varies by coverage type. For `JobFailure` and `SettlementDefault`, the Provider is the Assured Agent and the Client is the Beneficiary. For `EvaluatorDispute`, the Evaluator whose decision is being disputed serves as the Assured Agent (depositing collateral against the risk of erroneous evaluation), and the affected party (Client or Provider, depending on the direction of the dispute) serves as the Beneficiary. Implementations MUST ensure that the Assured Agent for an `EvaluatorDispute` JobAssurance holds a valid AssuranceAccount.

**Beneficiary**: The payout recipient explicitly designated by the Assured Agent at `commitToJob()` time. If the Assured Agent triggers a coverage condition, the Beneficiary is the sole party entitled to file a Claim against that JobAssurance, requesting a payout up to the `committedAmount`. Each JobAssurance is associated with exactly one Beneficiary, which cannot be changed once set.

**Claims Resolver**: A party authorized to adjudicate Claims, responsible for determining whether a Claim meets payout eligibility based on on-chain Job state.

In the core specification, the Assured Agent completes an assurance commitment for a specific Job by depositing collateral and calling `commitToJob()`. If the eligibility conditions for the covered risk are met, the Beneficiary may file a Claim against the corresponding JobAssurance. The Claims Resolver adjudicates the Claim based on eligibility conditions derivable from the covered Job's on-chain state and any on-chain attestation referenced by the Claim's evidence. Approved Claims are paid out from the Assured Agent's AssuranceAccount.

Implementations MAY define additional roles through optional extensions, such as challengers or governance actors.

### Scope

This specification defines:

- The `AssuranceAccount`, `JobAssurance`, and `Claim` primitives, including their data structures and state machines.
- The `CoverageType` enumeration for classifying risk categories.
- The `IAAP` core interface for depositing/withdrawing collateral, committing/releasing Job assurance, and filing/resolving/settling Claims.
- Required events.
- The integration model with [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Job states.

This specification does not define:

- Collateral utilization thresholds or LP withdrawal mechanisms.
- Collateral recommendation algorithms or pricing formulas.
- Governance parameters, voting mechanisms, or DAO structures.
- Specific compliance frameworks, KYC/KYB requirements, or AML screening implementations.
- Delegated funding models, third-party collateral provision, or profit-sharing mechanisms between Agents and external Backers.

### Assurance and Claims Lifecycle Overview

AAP divides fulfillment assurance into two layers: the account layer (AssuranceAccount) manages an Agent's global collateral reserve, and the Job layer (JobAssurance) manages commitment allocations against specific Jobs.

The typical flow is:

1. An Agent deposits collateral into the AAP contract; the AssuranceAccount enters the `Active` state.
2. The Agent calls `commitToJob()` for a specific Job, reserving `committedAmount` from `availableAmount` and creating an `Active` JobAssurance.
3. The Job executes. If the outcome meets the eligibility conditions, the counterparty files a Claim against that JobAssurance.
4. A qualified Claims Resolver adjudicates the Claim.
5. If approved, `approvedAmount` is debited from the AssuranceAccount and transferred to the Beneficiary; the JobAssurance transitions to `Paid`.
6. If the Job completes successfully, the Agent calls `releaseCommitment()`, returning `committedAmount` to `availableAmount`; the JobAssurance transitions to `Released`.

This design separates account-level asset management from Job-level assurance commitments and is both programmable and auditable.

### Claims Eligibility and Protection Model

AAP protects Agent commerce participants by binding claims to the lifecycle of a specific [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Job. AAP covers concrete commercial risks that can be adjudicated based on on-chain Job outcomes, with payouts sourced from the responsible Agent's own collateral.

Depending on the coverage type, AAP may protect against the following risks:

- Provider non-performance prior to completion
- Disputed Evaluator decisions
- Settlement failure after valid completion
- AML-related freezes affecting the covered Job (extension-reserved; requires `AMLFreeze` support)

Protection is based on concrete transaction and Job states, making Claims easier to audit and reducing dependence on subjective off-chain interpretation.

### Settlement Asset

Implementations MUST specify the ERC-20 settlement asset used for collateral deposits and claim payouts. This specification assumes a single settlement asset per AssuranceAccount. Cross-chain assurance is outside the scope of this version and is reserved for future extensions.

Implementations MUST NOT use ERC-20 tokens with fee-on-transfer or rebasing mechanisms as the settlement asset. Such tokens cause the actual amount received by the contract to differ from the `amount` parameter, breaking the `totalFunded == availableAmount + lockedAmount + paidOutAmount` invariant.

Implementations SHOULD use integer arithmetic throughout internal fund accounting to avoid rounding errors introduced by division. In cross-chain or Layer 2 environments where the settlement asset's `decimals` may differ from the mainnet, implementations MUST normalize incoming funds to the contract's internal accounting precision upon inflow to prevent dust accumulation from breaking the invariant.

### Coverage Types

```solidity
enum CoverageType {
    // Core types (MUST be supported)
    JobFailure,       // Provider non-performance; Job reached rejected or expired terminal state due to non-delivery
    EvaluatorDispute, // Evaluator decision challenged; requires formal dispute state or on-chain dispute attestation
    SettlementDefault,// Fund release failure after valid completion attestation, attributable to the Assured Agent

    // Extension-reserved types (OPTIONAL; defined here for enum stability across implementations)
    AMLFreeze,        // Agent or Beneficiary wallet flagged by AML screening, funds frozen during an in-progress Job
    SlashingLoss      // Collateral staked in an external protocol is slashed, reducing the Agent's effective backing
}
```

Implementations MUST support `JobFailure`, `EvaluatorDispute`, and `SettlementDefault`. `AMLFreeze` and `SlashingLoss` are extension-reserved: they are included in the enum to ensure consistent type encoding across implementations, but support for them is OPTIONAL. Implementations that do not support an extension-reserved type MUST revert if `commitToJob()` is called with that type.

`SlashingLoss` is intended for scenarios where an Agent's collateral is simultaneously staked in an external protocol (e.g., a restaking or validation network) and that protocol's slashing rules reduce the Agent's effective backing. Implementations that support `SlashingLoss` SHOULD define how the external slashing event is detected and attested on-chain.

### AssuranceAccount

Each `AssuranceAccount` corresponds to a single Assured Agent address and maintains its global collateral reserve. An Agent may hold at most one AssuranceAccount.

```solidity
struct AssuranceAccount {
    address agent;           // Assured Agent address
    address settlementAsset; // ERC-20 settlement asset
    uint256 totalFunded;     // Cumulative net inflow (deposits minus withdrawals); equals availableAmount + lockedAmount + paidOutAmount
    uint256 availableAmount; // Balance available for new commitments or withdrawal
    uint256 lockedAmount;    // Balance reserved for active JobAssurances (not withdrawable)
    uint256 paidOutAmount;   // Cumulative amount paid out to Beneficiaries
    AccountStatus status;
}
```

The invariant `totalFunded == availableAmount + lockedAmount + paidOutAmount` MUST hold after every state-mutating operation. `totalFunded` is not the contract's current token balance; it is the cumulative net inflow (total deposits minus total withdrawals). The contract's actual token balance at any point equals `availableAmount + lockedAmount`, since `paidOutAmount` has already been transferred out. This version does not account for external yield, additional slashing, or other out-of-account inflows; if such mechanisms are needed, implementations SHOULD define fund accounting rules through separate extensions.

Implementations MAY impose identity requirements on `agent`, for example through an [ERC-8004](./eip-8004.md)-compatible identity system. If identity requirements are imposed, `depositAssurance()` MUST revert when the caller does not satisfy them.

#### AssuranceAccount State Machine

```solidity
enum AccountStatus {
    Active, // Account is operational; deposits, withdrawals, and commitments are permitted
    Paused  // Implementation-defined pause state (e.g., during AML freeze); new commitments and withdrawals are not permitted
}
```

Valid state transitions:

```text
(initial) -> Active (Agent calls depositAssurance for the first time)
Active -> Paused (implementation-defined condition triggered, e.g., AML freeze)
Paused -> Active (implementation-defined recovery condition met)
```

Implementations MUST NOT allow state transitions other than those listed above. All state transitions MUST emit the corresponding event. An `availableAmount` of 0 is a numerical state of the account and does not constitute an independent state transition condition. Implementations SHOULD define a bounded recovery path from `Paused` to `Active` to prevent indefinite account lockout.

### JobAssurance

Each `JobAssurance` represents an assurance commitment by an Assured Agent for a specific [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Job. Each JobAssurance covers exactly one CoverageType and is associated with at most one Claim over its entire lifetime.

```solidity
struct JobAssurance {
    bytes32      assuranceId;
    address      assuredAgent;    // Corresponds to the AssuranceAccount's agent address
    address      beneficiary;     // Sole beneficiary designated at commitToJob; immutable
    bytes32      coveredJob;      // A single ERC-8183 jobId
    CoverageType coverageType;
    uint256      committedAmount; // Collateral reserved from availableAmount (settlement asset)
    uint64       expiry;          // After this timestamp the JobAssurance may be treated as Expired
    uint256      chainId;         // Reserved for future cross-chain extensions; MUST equal block.chainid in this version
    bytes32      claimId;         // Identifier of the associated Claim; bytes32(0) when no Claim has been filed
    AssuranceState state;
}
```

#### JobAssurance State Machine

```solidity
enum AssuranceState {
    Active,   // Assurance commitment is in effect; committedAmount has been reserved from availableAmount
    Claimed,  // A Claim has been filed against this JobAssurance; awaiting resolution
    Paid,     // Claim has been approved and payout completed
    Released, // Job completed successfully; committedAmount returned to availableAmount
    Expired   // Expired without a claim; committedAmount returned to availableAmount
}
```

Valid state transitions:

```text
Active  -> Claimed  (Beneficiary calls fileClaim)
Active  -> Released (Assured Agent calls releaseCommitment; Job has completed)
Active  -> Expired  (block.timestamp > expiry and no Claim filed)
Claimed -> Paid     (Claim approved and settled)
Claimed -> Active   (Claim denied; JobAssurance reverts to Active, awaiting subsequent release or expiry)
```

Implementations MUST NOT allow state transitions other than those listed above. All state transitions MUST emit the corresponding event.

The `Released` state represents only the path where the Assured Agent proactively returns the assurance commitment after successful Job completion. It does not cover post-denial release. When a Claim is denied, the JobAssurance reverts to `Active`, meaning the commitment remains locked and valid in both funds and state. This does not restore the right to file another Claim against that same JobAssurance; each JobAssurance is associated with at most one Claim over its entire lifetime. After reverting to `Active`, the Assured Agent may continue holding the commitment until proactively calling `releaseCommitment()` or until the assurance naturally expires.

#### JobAssurance Invariants

The following invariants MUST hold:

- A JobAssurance in `Active` or `Claimed` state: its `committedAmount` MUST be reflected in the corresponding AssuranceAccount's `lockedAmount`.
- A JobAssurance in `Released` or `Expired` state: its `committedAmount` MUST have been returned to `availableAmount`.
- A JobAssurance in `Paid` state: the associated Claim's `approvedAmount` MUST have been debited from the AssuranceAccount and credited to `paidOutAmount`.
- A JobAssurance in `Active` state that has never been claimed: its `claimId` MUST be `bytes32(0)`.
- A JobAssurance in `Active` state that has reverted from `Claimed` after denial: its `claimId` MUST retain the identifier of the denied Claim. This retained `claimId` serves as both an audit trail and a guard against duplicate filing; `fileClaim` MUST revert if `claimId != bytes32(0)`.
- A JobAssurance in `Claimed` or `Paid` state: its `claimId` MUST point to a valid Claim record.

### Claim

```solidity
struct Claim {
    bytes32    claimId;
    bytes32    assuranceId;      // The JobAssurance this Claim corresponds to
    address    beneficiary;      // The party filing the claim; MUST equal the beneficiary recorded in the corresponding JobAssurance
    uint256    requestedAmount;  // Requested payout amount; MUST be > 0 and <= committedAmount
    uint256    approvedAmount;   // If approved: > 0 and <= requestedAmount; if denied: 0
    ClaimState state;
    uint64     filedAt;          // Block timestamp when the Claim was filed
    uint64     resolvedAt;       // Block timestamp when the Claim was resolved (0 while pending)
}
```

#### Claim State Machine

```solidity
enum ClaimState {
    None,       // Storage default sentinel indicating that the claimId does not yet exist; not a lifecycle state of a created Claim
    Filed,      // Claim has been filed; awaiting resolution
    Challenged, // Resolution result has been challenged (optional; see extensions)
    Approved,   // Claim approved; awaiting settlement
    Denied,     // Claim denied
    Paid        // Payout completed
}
```

Valid state transitions:

```text
None       -> Filed      (Beneficiary calls fileClaim)
Filed      -> Approved   (Resolver approves)
Filed      -> Denied     (Resolver denies)
Filed      -> Challenged (Optional: challenged during the challenge window)
Challenged -> Approved   (Panel or DAO upholds/overturns to approve)
Challenged -> Denied     (Panel or DAO upholds/overturns to deny)
Approved   -> Paid       (payout called; settlement asset transferred from AssuranceAccount to Beneficiary)
```

Implementations MUST NOT allow state transitions other than those listed above. The `Challenged` state is OPTIONAL; implementations that do not support a challenge window MAY omit this state and its associated transitions. In such cases, `Filed` transitions directly to `Approved` or `Denied`.

If `payout()` is called on a Claim in the `Approved` state and the corresponding AssuranceAccount's `lockedAmount` is insufficient to cover the `approvedAmount`, `payout()` MUST revert. The Claim MUST remain in the `Approved` state until the AssuranceAccount has sufficient funds. Implementations MUST NOT introduce a `PartiallyPaid` state.

### Identifier Generation

Implementations MUST generate `assuranceId` and `claimId` values in a collision-resistant manner. To prevent namespace collisions between the two identifier types, implementations SHOULD include a distinct domain separator for each, for example `keccak256(abi.encodePacked("AAP_ASSURANCE", agent, jobId, coverageType, nonce))` for assurance identifiers and `keccak256(abi.encodePacked("AAP_CLAIM", assuranceId, nonce))` for claim identifiers, where `nonce` is a monotonically increasing counter maintained by the contract. The specific generation scheme is implementation-defined, but identifiers MUST be unique within a single contract deployment and SHOULD be deterministically reproducible from their inputs to support off-chain indexing and cross-implementation tooling.

### Core Interface

Implementations MUST conform to the following interface:

```solidity
interface IAAP {

    // ---- AssuranceAccount lifecycle ----

    /// @notice Agent deposits collateral to register or top up an AssuranceAccount.
    /// @dev If the Agent does not yet have an AssuranceAccount, one is created and enters the Active state.
    /// If an account already exists, the deposit is added to availableAmount.
    /// The caller MUST have approved the AAP contract to transfer the specified amount.
    /// Implementations MAY impose identity requirements on the caller (e.g., through an
    /// [ERC-8004](./eip-8004.md)-compatible system); if imposed, depositAssurance MUST revert
    /// when the caller does not satisfy those requirements.
    /// This version requires msg.sender to be the Assured Agent.
    /// Future extensions MAY introduce a depositFor(address agent, uint256 amount) variant
    /// to support delegated funding by third-party Backers.
    /// @param amount The amount of settlement asset to deposit.
    function depositAssurance(uint256 amount) external;

    /// @notice Agent withdraws available balance from the AssuranceAccount.
    /// @dev Only the availableAmount portion may be withdrawn; lockedAmount is not withdrawable.
    /// The Agent MAY withdraw up to the full availableAmount regardless of whether
    /// Active or Claimed JobAssurances exist, since those commitments are already
    /// accounted for in lockedAmount. If amount exceeds availableAmount, MUST revert.
    /// @param amount The amount of settlement asset to withdraw; pass type(uint256).max to withdraw the full available balance.
    function withdrawAvailableAssurance(uint256 amount) external;

    /// @notice Query the AssuranceAccount details for a given Agent.
    function getAssuranceAccount(address agent) external view returns (AssuranceAccount memory);

    // ---- JobAssurance lifecycle ----

    /// @notice Agent creates an assurance commitment for a specific Job.
    /// @dev The caller is treated as the assuredAgent.
    /// MUST deduct amount from the caller's availableAmount and credit it to lockedAmount.
    /// If availableAmount is insufficient, MUST revert.
    /// If the covered Job's coverage condition already qualifies for a claim, MUST revert.
    /// If the AssuranceAccount is not in Active state, MUST revert.
    /// If an Active or Claimed JobAssurance already exists for the same (jobId, coverageType) combination, MUST revert.
    /// chainId MUST be set to block.chainid by the implementation; this version does not support cross-chain assurance.
    /// The field is reserved for future extensions; cross-chain verification would be defined through a separate ERC.
    /// @param jobId The ERC-8183 Job identifier.
    /// @param coverageType The risk type to cover.
    /// @param beneficiary The sole address entitled to file a Claim against this JobAssurance.
    /// @param amount The collateral amount to commit from availableAmount.
    /// @param expiry The expiration timestamp for this JobAssurance.
    /// @return assuranceId The unique identifier of the newly created JobAssurance.
    function commitToJob(
        bytes32 jobId,
        CoverageType coverageType,
        address beneficiary,
        uint256 amount,
        uint64 expiry
    ) external returns (bytes32 assuranceId);

    /// @notice Agent releases the assurance commitment for a completed Job, returning committedAmount to availableAmount.
    /// @dev If the caller is not the assuredAgent of this JobAssurance, MUST revert.
    /// If the JobAssurance is not in Active state, MUST revert.
    /// If the corresponding ERC-8183 Job has not reached a successful completion terminal state, MUST revert.
    /// For the purposes of this specification, a "successful completion terminal state" means
    /// the Job has been delivered, evaluated, and settled (or equivalent attestation exists on-chain).
    /// Mutual cancellation, abandonment, or other non-success terminal states do not qualify;
    /// in such cases the Assured Agent MUST wait for the JobAssurance to expire via expireCommitment().
    /// MUST return committedAmount from lockedAmount to availableAmount.
    /// MUST transition the JobAssurance to Released.
    /// @param assuranceId The JobAssurance to release.
    function releaseCommitment(bytes32 assuranceId) external;

    /// @notice Trigger expiration of a JobAssurance that has exceeded its expiry timestamp.
    /// @dev MAY be called by any address.
    /// If the JobAssurance is not in Active state, MUST revert.
    /// If block.timestamp <= expiry, MUST revert.
    /// MUST return committedAmount from lockedAmount to availableAmount.
    /// MUST transition the JobAssurance to Expired and emit AssuranceExpired.
    /// Implementations MAY also perform lazy expiry checks within other state-mutating functions;
    /// this explicit function ensures a canonical on-chain trigger path exists.
    /// @param assuranceId The JobAssurance to expire.
    function expireCommitment(bytes32 assuranceId) external;

    /// @notice Query the details of a given JobAssurance.
    function getJobAssurance(bytes32 assuranceId) external view returns (JobAssurance memory);

    // ---- Claim lifecycle ----

    /// @notice File a Claim against an Active JobAssurance.
    /// @dev If the JobAssurance is not in Active state, MUST revert.
    /// If the caller is not the beneficiary recorded in the JobAssurance, MUST revert.
    /// If the JobAssurance's claimId is not bytes32(0), MUST revert.
    /// This covers both prior denial (claimId retained) and prior payout (state would not be Active).
    /// If requestedAmount is 0 or exceeds committedAmount, MUST revert.
    /// MUST verify that the corresponding ERC-8183 eligibility conditions have been met on-chain.
    /// MUST transition the JobAssurance to Claimed, create a Claim in Filed state,
    /// and write the new Claim's claimId into JobAssurance.claimId.
    /// Each JobAssurance is associated with at most one Claim over its lifetime;
    /// if a subsequent claim is needed, the Assured Agent must first release or allow expiry of the
    /// current JobAssurance, then call commitToJob() again to create a new one.
    /// @param assuranceId The JobAssurance to claim against.
    /// @param requestedAmount The requested payout amount in settlement asset.
    /// @param evidence Supporting evidence (IPFS CID, ZK proof, or attestation reference).
    /// @return claimId The unique identifier of the newly filed Claim.
    function fileClaim(
        bytes32 assuranceId,
        uint256 requestedAmount,
        bytes calldata evidence
    ) external returns (bytes32 claimId);

    /// @notice Resolve a pending Claim.
    /// @dev If the caller is not a qualified Claims Resolver, MUST revert.
    /// If the Claim is not in Filed state (or Challenged state if supported), MUST revert.
    /// The Resolver MUST base approval or denial on eligibility conditions derivable from
    /// the covered Job's on-chain state and any on-chain attestation referenced by the Claim's evidence.
    /// If approved, MUST set approvedAmount to > 0 and <= requestedAmount, and transition the Claim to Approved.
    /// If denied, MUST set the stored value to 0, transition the Claim to Denied,
    /// and revert the JobAssurance to Active; the JobAssurance's claimId MUST NOT be cleared,
    /// ensuring the denied Claim remains traceable and preventing duplicate filing.
    /// committedAmount remains in lockedAmount and the assurance commitment stays in effect.
    /// @param claimId The Claim to resolve.
    /// @param approved Whether the Claim is approved.
    /// @param approvedAmount The approved payout amount in settlement asset.
    ///        When approved == false, this parameter is ignored;
    ///        when approved == true, MUST be > 0 and <= requestedAmount.
    /// @param reason Supporting rationale (IPFS CID or on-chain bytes).
    function resolveClaim(
        bytes32 claimId,
        bool approved,
        uint256 approvedAmount,
        bytes calldata reason
    ) external;

    /// @notice Settle an approved Claim by transferring settlement asset from AssuranceAccount to the Beneficiary.
    /// @dev MAY be called by any address. Permissionless settlement allows keeper bots,
    /// the Beneficiary, or the Claims Resolver to trigger payout without access restrictions.
    /// If the Claim is not in Approved state, MUST revert.
    /// If the AssuranceAccount's lockedAmount is insufficient, MUST revert.
    /// MUST debit approvedAmount from lockedAmount, credit it to paidOutAmount, and transfer to the Beneficiary.
    /// MUST transition the Claim to Paid and the JobAssurance to Paid.
    /// @param claimId The approved Claim to settle.
    function payout(bytes32 claimId) external;

    // ---- Queries ----

    /// @notice Query a Claim by its identifier.
    function getClaim(bytes32 claimId) external view returns (Claim memory);
}
```

### Integration with ERC-8183

AAP implementations MUST determine Claim eligibility based on verifiable [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Job state or equivalent on-chain attestation. The mechanism used to read Job state (event monitoring, direct contract queries, oracle attestation) is left to the implementation, but eligibility determination MUST be grounded in verifiable on-chain data.

The following table defines the claimable conditions for each CoverageType:

| ERC-8183 Condition | AAP Coverage Type | Claim Eligibility |
|--------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Provider non-performance; Job reached rejected or expired terminal state due to non-delivery | `JobFailure` | Claim is eligible |
| Settlement failure after valid completion attestation, attributable to the Assured Agent (e.g., Agent refuses to execute settlement); settlement failures caused by third-party contract defects or platform infrastructure outages are excluded | `SettlementDefault` | Claim is eligible |
| Covered Job has entered a formal dispute state defined by the underlying commerce protocol, or an on-chain dispute attestation recognized by the AAP implementation has been posted | `EvaluatorDispute` | Claim is eligible |
| Beneficiary or provider wallet is flagged by AML after the Job enters Funded state (optional) | `AMLFreeze` | Claim is eligible |

Settlement failures caused by third-party contract defects or platform infrastructure outages are explicitly outside the scope of `SettlementDefault`; such cases should be handled through independent mechanisms.

`SettlementDefault` does not cover ordinary provider non-performance before valid completion conditions are met; such cases fall under `JobFailure`.

If the underlying [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Job is terminated by mutual agreement (neither a failure nor a successful completion), no CoverageType's eligibility condition is met, and the Beneficiary cannot file a Claim. Since mutual cancellation is not a successful completion terminal state, `releaseCommitment()` MUST revert in this scenario; the Assured Agent MUST wait for the JobAssurance to expire naturally via `expireCommitment()`.

Implementations MUST validate eligibility conditions at the time `fileClaim` is called.

AAP integrates with [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) through read-only observation; it does not require modifications to [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) contracts.

### Optional Extensions

All interfaces below are OPTIONAL. Implementations MAY adopt any combination of these extensions.

#### IRiskHook (Collateral Recommendation)

Implementations MAY register an `IRiskHook` to dynamically recommend minimum commitment amounts based on Agent reputation, historical completion rate, or compliance signals.

```solidity
interface IRiskHook {
    /// @notice Recommend an assurance amount for a given Agent, coverage type, and Job value.
    /// @dev MAY read from ERC-8004-compatible identity, reputation, or compliance systems to assess risk.
    /// @param agent The Agent address being queried.
    /// @param cType The coverage type being evaluated.
    /// @param jobValue The settlement asset value of the ERC-8183 Job.
    /// @return recommendedAmount The recommended assurance commitment amount.
    function computeRecommendedAmount(
        address agent,
        CoverageType cType,
        uint256 jobValue
    ) external view returns (uint256 recommendedAmount);
}
```

#### IAMLHook (AML Screening)

Implementations that support the `AMLFreeze` coverage type SHOULD implement the `IAMLHook` interface.

```solidity
interface IAMLHook {
    /// @notice Screen an Agent before collateral deposit.
    function screenAgent(address agent) external view returns (bool passed, string memory reason);

    /// @notice Notify the implementation that an AML freeze condition has been detected, triggering AssuranceAccount Paused state.
    function onAMLFreeze(address agent) external;

    /// @notice Notify the implementation that the AML freeze condition has been lifted, restoring AssuranceAccount Active state.
    function onAMLUnfreeze(address agent) external;
}
```

Implementations SHOULD block `withdrawAvailableAssurance` and `commitToJob` during an AML freeze and restore these permissions after the freeze is lifted.

#### Multi-Resolver Panel Adjudication

Implementations MAY require multiple Claims Resolvers to adjudicate a Claim. The number of resolvers and the approval threshold are implementation-defined.

#### Resolver Staking and Slashing

Implementations MAY require Claims Resolvers to stake settlement assets as a bond for honest adjudication. If a resolution is overturned, a portion of the resolver's stake MAY be slashed. Slashed funds MAY flow to an implementation-defined destination.

#### Optimistic Resolution

Implementations MAY adopt an optimistic resolution model for low-value Claims:

1. A single Claims Resolver issues an initial resolution.
2. The resolution enters a challenge window (recommended no less than 72 hours).
3. Any address that meets the implementation-defined challenge eligibility requirements MAY initiate a challenge by posting a challenge bond.
4. If no challenge is raised, the resolution is automatically finalized.
5. If challenged, the Claim escalates to panel adjudication or DAO review.
6. The losing party's bond or stake is slashed.

Implementations that support optimistic resolution MUST implement the `Challenged` state in the Claim state machine.

#### Recusal Rules

To prevent conflicts of interest arising from identity overlap between the Claims Resolver and the [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Evaluator:

- If `coverageType` is `EvaluatorDispute`, any address that served as the [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Evaluator for the covered Job MUST NOT also serve as the Claims Resolver for that Claim. Implementations MUST enforce this restriction at resolution time.
- For other coverage types, recusal is recommended but not mandatory, since non-dispute Claims can typically be adjudicated based solely on on-chain state.

#### Resolver Accuracy Tracking

Implementations MAY maintain a historical accuracy score for each Claims Resolver (number of non-overturned resolutions / total resolutions). Such data MAY be published to an [ERC-8004](./eip-8004.md)-compatible reputation or compliance system.

#### Genesis Resolver Set

During the cold-start phase, implementers MAY designate a Genesis Resolver Set as the initial authorized resolvers at deployment. Genesis Resolvers MUST be subject to the same staking, challenge, and accuracy tracking rules as any subsequent resolver. Implementers SHOULD publish the Genesis Resolver Set members, selection rationale, and a timeline for opening the Resolver pool to external participants. Detailed admission criteria, transition mechanisms, and exit procedures are deployment concerns and SHOULD be documented outside this specification.

#### Delegated Funding (Reserved)

This version assumes all collateral is self-funded by the Assured Agent. Future extensions MAY introduce a delegated funding model with third-party Backers; the delegated funding lifecycle, profit distribution, and exit mechanics are expected to be specified in a separate ERC. Implementations that anticipate delegated funding SHOULD design deposit accounting to distinguish self-funded from delegated capital.

## Rationale

**Two-layer structure: Account-level + Job-level.** AssuranceAccount manages an Agent's global collateral reserve. JobAssurance manages commitment allocations for specific Jobs. Separating the two avoids the ambiguity of conflating account balance with Job-level commitments in concurrent Job scenarios, while allowing an Agent to provide assurance for multiple concurrent Jobs from a single account.

**Released as a distinct terminal state.** When a Job completes successfully, `committedAmount` should be returned to `availableAmount` rather than handled through the denial path. The `Released` state clearly distinguishes "commitment returned after successful fulfillment" from "commitment released after claim denial," preventing the normal success path from being conflated with the denial path.

**Independent Claim primitive.** Extracting `Claim` from `JobAssurance` as an independent first-class primitive with its own state machine supports richer adjudication flows without overloading the JobAssurance state transitions.

**Revert to Active on denial.** When a Claim is denied, the JobAssurance reverts to `Active` and `committedAmount` remains in `lockedAmount`. A denied Claim does not imply successful Job completion. The evidence may have been insufficient, the filing premature, or the eligibility conditions unmet, yet the Job may still genuinely fail later. Keeping the commitment in effect until the Assured Agent proactively calls `releaseCommitment()` or the assurance naturally expires prevents premature release of the commitment and protects the Beneficiary's coverage.

**Retained claimId after denial.** When a Claim is denied and the JobAssurance reverts to `Active`, the `claimId` field is intentionally not cleared. This serves two purposes: it preserves an on-chain audit trail linking the denied Claim to the JobAssurance, and it acts as a guard preventing a second `fileClaim` against the same JobAssurance. If a subsequent claim is needed after denial, the Assured Agent must first release or allow expiry of the current JobAssurance, then create a new one via `commitToJob()`.

**Single Claim per JobAssurance.** Each JobAssurance permits at most one Claim over its lifetime. This is a deliberate anti-spam design choice, not an incidental consequence of the state machine. It encourages Beneficiaries to ensure that on-chain eligibility conditions are fully met before filing, reducing speculative or premature claims and lowering the adjudication burden on Claims Resolvers. The tradeoff is that a Beneficiary whose Claim is denied cannot retry against the same JobAssurance. Because a denied JobAssurance reverts to `Active` and the duplicate-commitment rule prevents creating a new JobAssurance for the same `(jobId, coverageType)` while an `Active` one exists, continued coverage requires the Assured Agent to first release the prior assurance via `releaseCommitment()` (if the Job has reached a successful terminal state) or wait for it to expire via `expireCommitment()`, and then call `commitToJob()` again. This sequencing is intentional: it prevents two concurrent assurance commitments for the same risk from coexisting.

**Explicit expireCommitment function.** Without a standardized trigger, the `Active -> Expired` transition exists in the state machine but has no canonical on-chain execution path. Different implementations could use lazy evaluation, keeper bots, or manual calls, making interoperability testing ambiguous. `expireCommitment()` provides a permissionless, explicit trigger while still allowing implementations to perform lazy expiry checks in other functions.

**totalFunded as cumulative net inflow.** `totalFunded` tracks cumulative deposits minus cumulative withdrawals, not the contract's current token balance. The contract's actual token holdings at any point equal `availableAmount + lockedAmount`, since `paidOutAmount` has already been transferred out. This accounting model was chosen so that `totalFunded` monotonically reflects the Agent's historical funding commitment, which is useful for external reputation and risk systems that consume on-chain data.

**Strict insolvency semantics.** When `lockedAmount` is insufficient, `payout()` reverts rather than performing a partial payout. This avoids introducing a `PartiallyPaid` state that would create ambiguity around obligations. The core protocol's stance on insolvency is to preserve invariants over liveness: a Claim stuck in `Approved` state is preferable to a settlement that violates the accounting model. Implementations that introduce extensions capable of consuming `lockedAmount` (such as external slashing) MUST also provide a fallback funding mechanism or a defined resolution path (e.g., a reinsurance pool or a timeout-based Defaulted terminal state) to prevent Claims from remaining indefinitely stuck in `Approved` without settlement.

**Core/extension separation.** The core specification defines only what is necessary for interoperability. This continues the design philosophy of [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md): define the minimum surface and delegate complex business logic to hooks and extensions.

**Chain-agnostic specification.** This ERC does not mandate a specific deployment chain or settlement asset. Reference implementations MAY use USDC on Base for operational simplicity and lower transaction costs.

**Boundary between JobFailure and SettlementDefault.** `JobFailure` covers provider non-delivery, including both explicit rejection and timeout due to non-submission. `SettlementDefault` covers only post-completion blockage where the Assured Agent bears responsibility for the settlement step, for example, where the Agent holds the settlement key but refuses to execute, or where an Agent-controlled contract fails to trigger payment as agreed. If the settlement failure is caused by third-party contract defects, platform infrastructure outages, or other causes not attributable to the Assured Agent, it falls outside the scope of `SettlementDefault`. Such cases should be handled through independent mechanisms.

**AMLFreeze as optional coverage.** AML enforcement is highly dependent on jurisdiction and oracle infrastructure. Making it a core requirement would impose additional burden on implementations targeting permissionless environments.

**Genesis Resolver Set cold-start design.** A decentralized Resolver pool takes time to form. The Genesis Resolver Set is a time-limited transitional arrangement that ensures early adjudication availability while requiring equal staking, challenge, and accuracy rules for all resolvers from day one.

## Backwards Compatibility

This ERC introduces new contracts and interfaces and does not modify the behavior of [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md), [ERC-8004](./eip-8004.md), or any existing ERC. Whether existing [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Jobs are eligible for Claims is implementation-defined. Implementations SHOULD NOT allow retroactive creation of JobAssurances against already-completed Jobs unless additional verification mechanisms are in place to prevent adverse selection.

AAP integrates with [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) through read-only observation and does not require modifications to [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) contracts. Integration with [ERC-8004](./eip-8004.md)-compatible systems is accomplished through implementation-defined queries; writing resolver accuracy data back to such systems is an extension concern.

## Test Cases

Test cases will be published alongside the reference implementation. Key scenarios to validate include:

1. **Full happy path (assurance path)**: `depositAssurance` -> `commitToJob` -> Job completes -> `releaseCommitment`; `committedAmount` returns to `availableAmount`; JobAssurance transitions to Released.
2. **Full happy path (claims path)**: `depositAssurance` -> `commitToJob` -> Job fails -> `fileClaim` -> `resolveClaim(approved)` -> `payout`; `approvedAmount` debited from `lockedAmount`.
3. **Denial path**: `fileClaim` -> `resolveClaim(denied)`; Claim transitions to Denied; JobAssurance reverts to Active; `committedAmount` remains in `lockedAmount`; a subsequent `fileClaim` against the same JobAssurance MUST revert; a new `commitToJob` for the same `(jobId, coverageType)` MUST revert while the prior JobAssurance is Active; renewal requires release or expiry first.
4. **Expiry path**: `commitToJob` -> time exceeds expiry -> any party calls `expireCommitment` -> JobAssurance transitions to Expired; `committedAmount` returned.
5. **Premature expiry**: Calling `expireCommitment` before expiry MUST revert.
6. **Insolvency**: `resolveClaim(approved)` succeeds but `payout` reverts due to insufficient `lockedAmount`; Claim remains Approved.
7. **Withdrawal boundary**: `withdrawAvailableAssurance` succeeds up to `availableAmount`; attempting to withdraw any portion of `lockedAmount` MUST revert.
8. **Invalid state transitions**: Filing a Claim against a non-Active JobAssurance MUST revert; calling payout on a non-Approved Claim MUST revert.
9. **Eligibility gating**: If the covered Job has not reached the required terminal state, `fileClaim` MUST revert.
10. **Adverse selection**: If coverage conditions already qualify for a claim, `commitToJob` MUST revert.
11. **Duplicate commitment**: If an Active or Claimed JobAssurance already exists for the same (jobId, coverageType) combination, a subsequent `commitToJob` MUST revert.
12. **Amount validation**: `fileClaim` with `requestedAmount == 0` MUST revert; `resolveClaim(approved)` with `approvedAmount == 0` MUST revert.
13. **Recusal rule enforcement**: If `resolveClaim` for an `EvaluatorDispute` Claim is called by the [ERC-8183](./eip-8183.md) Evaluator of that Job, it MUST revert.
14. **Invariant verification**: After any operation, `totalFunded == availableAmount + lockedAmount + paidOutAmount` MUST hold.

## Security Considerations

**Defensive check for payout() insolvency.** Under normal flow, `commitToJob()` has already deducted `committedAmount` from `availableAmount` and credited it to `lockedAmount`, and `approvedAmount <= requestedAmount <= committedAmount`, so `lockedAmount` should theoretically never be insufficient. The insolvency check in `payout()` is a defensive measure to cover the following edge cases: an optional extension that introduces external slashing against `lockedAmount`, or an underlying contract anomaly in the settlement asset. In a base implementation without such extensions, this check should never trigger.

The core specification intentionally does not introduce partial payout or timeout degradation mechanisms at this point, to avoid creating ambiguity around obligations and additional state machine complexity. If an implementer introduces extensions that may consume `lockedAmount` (such as a slashing mechanism), it MUST also design a complementary fallback payout source or reinsurance mechanism to ensure the Beneficiary retains an executable payout path under extreme conditions and to prevent Claims from remaining stuck in the `Approved` state indefinitely without settlement.

**Adverse selection and timing attacks.** If JobAssurance creation is allowed after a Job has already reached a claimable terminal state, the implementation is exposed to retroactive arbitrage risk. `commitToJob()` MUST validate at execution time that the covered risk does not already qualify for a claim.

**Withdrawal race.** An Agent may attempt to withdraw `availableAmount` while Claims are pending to avoid payout obligations. The implementation addresses this by crediting `committedAmount` to `lockedAmount`, ensuring sufficient payout funds. `withdrawAvailableAssurance` MUST NOT allow withdrawal of the `lockedAmount` portion.

**Reentrancy.** `payout()` and `withdrawAvailableAssurance()` transfer ERC-20 tokens to external addresses. Implementations MUST follow the checks-effects-interactions pattern: update all internal accounting state (deducting from `lockedAmount` or `availableAmount`, crediting `paidOutAmount`, transitioning Claim/JobAssurance states) before executing the external `transfer` call.

**Concurrent Claims overcommitment.** If the same AssuranceAccount has multiple active JobAssurances, the sum of all `committedAmount` values may exceed the balance from any single deposit. Implementations MUST synchronously deduct `committedAmount` from `availableAmount` at `commitToJob` time to ensure `lockedAmount` is backed by actual funds.

**Claims Resolver authority.** In a single-resolver configuration, the Claims Resolver has unilateral influence over Claim outcomes. Implementations should employ staking, challenge windows, or panel adjudication (see optional extensions) to mitigate this risk.

**Freshness and manipulability of external risk inputs.** If an implementation relies on external reputation systems or compliance signals, it should define freshness requirements, update frequency, and failure handling rules to reduce the risk of using stale, manipulable, or selectively available inputs.

**AML screening oracle dependency.** `IAMLHook` implementations depend on external data sources. Compromised or exploited oracles may incorrectly flag Agents. Implementations should use multiple independent providers and handle false positives through `onAMLUnfreeze`.

**Resolver stake sizing.** If a resolver's stake is too small relative to the Claim amount, the economic incentive for honest adjudication may be insufficient. Implementations should set staking requirements tiered by the maximum adjudicable amount.

**On-chain attribution limitations of SettlementDefault.** `SettlementDefault` covers only settlement failures attributable to the Assured Agent, but on-chain data alone often cannot objectively distinguish "intentional obstruction" from "unexpected underlying contract failure." To maintain the principle that eligibility determination MUST be grounded in verifiable on-chain data, implementations SHOULD require attribution evidence to be submitted as an on-chain attestation (e.g., a signed attestation from a recognized attestor, or a verifiable proof posted to an attestation registry) rather than raw off-chain evidence. The `evidence` parameter in `fileClaim()` SHOULD contain a reference to such an on-chain attestation. The burden of proof rests with the Beneficiary; the attestation format and accepted attestors are implementation-defined. If cross-chain assurance is supported in the future, a secure cross-chain Job state verification mechanism must be defined (e.g., cross-chain messaging protocols or attestation bridges).
